Data visualization

A weight chart is emotional. A single heavy morning shouldn't read as failure, and a good week shouldn't be buried in noise. Aurora's charts are built to tell the truth about the trend — calm, honest and always anchored to the goal — using nothing but hand-written SVG.

Four principles

−3.5 kg
Daily 7-day average Range band Goal 73.0 kg
  • Trend over points. The daily line is deliberately quiet; the 7-day average is the confident, thicker read. People should see the direction of travel, not fixate on one weigh-in.
  • The goal is always visible. A dashed brand line sits in every chart. Progress only means something relative to a target, so the target is never off-screen.
  • Annotate meaning, not noise. Mark milestones, plateaus and events — a marked point should have a reason. Never label every data point.
  • Draw on entry. The line animates in once, then holds perfectly still. Under prefers-reduced-motion it simply appears.

Moving averages

Body weight swings a kilo a day on water alone. A 7-day moving average filters that noise into the signal people actually care about. Draw the raw daily series thin and low-contrast; draw the average thicker on top. The average — not any single dot — is what the eye should land on.

viz/moving-average
Daily reading (noise) 7-day average (signal)
Read the average, judge the trend. When someone asks “am I losing weight?”, they mean the average line's slope — never the gap between two adjacent mornings. Copy and callouts should always quote the averaged figure.

Choosing visualization colors

Series colors come from a fixed, ordered ramp — --viz-1 through --viz-5 — tuned for contrast in both themes. Assign them in order and stop when you run out; if a chart needs a sixth color, it needs fewer series. Semantic meaning always wins: the primary weight series is teal because down is good, and the goal is brand gold.

Grid & axis use --viz-grid / --viz-axis — barely-there, never competing with the data.
  • Teal (--viz-2) is the weight line — the "on track / loss is good" color, used for the primary daily series.
  • Gold (--viz-1) carries the average and anything brand-critical, like the goal marker.
  • Coral (--viz-5) appears only for regressions or over-goal states — never as a neutral fifth category.
  • Never encode a category with color alone — pair it with a legend, a direct label, or a distinct line style.

Dial vs line vs sparkline

Three shapes, three questions. Pick the one that matches what the person is asking at that moment — don't reach for a chart when a number and a goal will do.

73%

Dial

“How close am I?” One value against one goal. The hero of the dashboard — use exactly one per view.

Line

“Which way am I heading?” A series over time, with average, band and goal. The detail view.

Sparkline

“At a glance?” A label-free micro-trend for a card or row. Shape only — no axes, no goal.

Do & don't

Do
One measure, one axis, goal in view, average leading.
Don't
No dual-axis charts and no rainbow of series — spaghetti hides the trend.
Accessibility. Every chart is decorative SVG, so it carries role="img" and an aria-label that states the trend in words (“down 3.5 kg over 90 days, 1.9 kg above goal”). Meaning is never left to color alone — line weight, dashes and direct labels do the work too, and the whole thing prints and renders identically offline.

Navigation

The surfaces that move people between top-level destinations. A glass top bar for context and actions, a thumb-friendly bottom bar on mobile, and a side rail on wider screens — each marking exactly one active place in gold.

When to use it

Navigation carries the top-level destinations of the app — the places someone returns to again and again. It’s persistent, predictable, and adapts to the viewport: bottom bar on phones, side rail on desktops.

  • Keep the set small and stable: 3–5 destinations, each an icon plus a one-word label.
  • Exactly one item is active, colored in brand gold; the rest stay muted.
  • Use the top bar for the current screen’s title and its one or two contextual actions — not for navigation between sections.

Mobile vs. desktop

The same destinations render through two components. On phones the .au-bottomnav keeps targets within thumb reach and respects the device’s safe-area inset. From tablet width up, the .au-siderail gives each destination a full label and room to grow. Swap between them at your layout breakpoint; never show both at once.

Choosing a surface

SurfaceViewportCarries
.au-bottomnavPhone3–5 top-level destinations, within thumb reach.

.au-topbarAllScreen title and its contextual actions — not section switching.
Accessibility. Wrap the destinations in a <nav> with an aria-label, and mark the current item aria-current="page" in addition to .is-active — color alone can’t signal the active state. Always pair each icon with a visible text label; targets meet the 44 px minimum and keep the built-in focus ring.
